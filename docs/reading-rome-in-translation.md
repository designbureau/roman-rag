# Reading Rome in Translation

*On the originals, the public-domain English, and what a translation keeps
and loses.*

---

## 1. The arrangement

The Roman Archive presents each work in translation, and, where it survives
in the corpus, beside its original. Cicero's letters to Atticus appear as
parallel text: the Latin of L. C. Purser's Oxford edition (1903) on one side,
the English of E. S. Shuckburgh (1899–1900) on the other. Caesar's
commentaries and Seneca's letters carry their Latin likewise; Augustus's
*Res Gestae* stands beside the Latin recovered from its inscriptions. Marcus
Aurelius's *Meditations*, written in Greek, reaches you for now in the
English of Meric Casaubon alone.

The arrangement looks neutral (two columns, one meaning), but no translation
is a transparent pane of glass, and a reading interface that retrieves and
quotes from the English column owes its readers a clear account of what that
column is. This essay gives that account: what the English gives, who each
translator was, what each rendering loses, and what sits beneath the Latin
column itself.

## 2. What the English gives

For a reader without fluent Latin or Greek, the translation is the difference
between a work being legible and being a locked room. It carries the sense:
who is being asked what, which deed is being claimed, which consolation is
being reached for. And where the original sits alongside, a reader with a
little of the language can check the English against it phrase by phrase,
the slow, comparative reading these texts reward, and the reading the
parallel display is built to invite.

There is a further, structural point. The Archive's retrieval runs over the
English (the embeddings are of the translation, not the original), so the
translation is not merely what you read; it is what the system searches. The
translator's choices set the boundary of what a query can find. That makes
the question "whose English is this?" a technical question as well as a
literary one.

Translation studies has a name for the tell-tale residue this leaves.
Translated text is not linguistically identical to text originally composed
in the target language: it carries systematic traces of the source, and of
translation as a process, that make "translationese" measurable: the
tendency to normalise, to explicitate what the original left implicit, and to
shadow the source's structure (Baker 1993; Volansky, Ordan & Wintner 2013).
For a reader this is a matter of texture; for a retrieval system it is a
matter of recall. A query embedded in ordinary modern English is being
matched against text whose vocabulary and phrasing were pulled, to a
measurable degree, toward a Latin or Greek original and toward the idiom of
the translator's own century. The mediation the rest of this essay describes
in literary terms is, from the system's side, a bias in the embedding space
itself.

## 3. Each translator has a date and a temperament

The Archive's translations were not chosen by literary judgement; they were
chosen by copyright. What is freely available is the public-domain stratum,
and that stratum has a shape: it runs from the 1630s to the 1920s, with one
modern exception. It is worth knowing who these people were:

- **Meric Casaubon (1634)** made the first English translation of the
  *Meditations*, a generation before Pepys. It is a magnificent period piece:
  expansive, sermonic, often closer to paraphrase than translation, with
  seventeenth-century theology audible under the Stoicism. Casaubon's Marcus
  is further from the Greek than any other rendering in the Archive is from
  its Latin, and his prose is also the finest thing in it.
- **William Melmoth (1746, revised by F. C. T. Bosanquet)** turned Pliny's
  polished letters into the polite English of the Georgian essay. Famously
  elegant, famously free: Melmoth smooths, expands, and "improves," and the
  revision only partly reins him in.
- **William Duncan (whose Caesar the Archive carries in an 1856 printing)**
  and **W. A. McDevitte & W. S. Bohn (1870–1872)** render the commentaries in
  the workmanlike English of the schoolroom crib: accurate at the level of
  the clause, tone-deaf to Caesar's studied plainness, which was itself a
  rhetorical performance.
- **E. S. Shuckburgh (1899–1900)** translated the whole surviving
  correspondence of Cicero in four volumes: dignified, reliable Victorian
  prose, still the standard free English of the letters.
- **R. M. Gummere (1917–1925)** and **H. E. Butler (1920–1922)** are early
  Loeb Classical Library volumes (Seneca and Quintilian respectively), from
  the era when the Loebs aimed at serviceable facing-page English rather than
  literary character.
- **Thomas Bushnell (1998)**, the one modern voice, translated the
  *Res Gestae* and released it under a free licence, which is the only
  reason the Archive's newest translation is newer than 1925.

Each writes with the idiom of their own century: when Marcus Aurelius answers
a question in this Archive, the words carry a faint accent of 1634, and Pliny
arrives sounding like 1746. The Archive names the translator on every
citation because the Englishness a reader hears is Casaubon's or Melmoth's,
and it must never be mistaken for the author's.

## 4. What the English loses

The losses differ by author, because each author's Latin (or Greek) does
different work:

- **Cicero's letters** are the most informal Latin prose to survive
  antiquity: elliptical, allusive, full of Greek code-switching. He drops
  into Greek for irony, intimacy, or discretion, much as a bilingual
  correspondent does today. Shuckburgh renders the Greek and the Latin into
  one continuous English, and that seam, visible in the original as a change
  of alphabet, disappears. The puns mostly evaporate too. And in his speeches
  and treatises Cicero composed for the ear, ending periods on rhythmic
  clausulae so regular that they can be studied statistically (Zieliński
  1904); no translation carries prose rhythm across.
- **Caesar's plainness is a performance.** The third person ("Caesar
  ordered…"), the spare vocabulary, the unhurried syntax in the middle of
  catastrophe: this is a general writing his own dispatches as though an
  impartial historian were following him around (Riggsby 2006). A flat
  English translation reproduces the plainness but not the artfulness of it;
  it reads as mere report, which is exactly what Caesar wanted readers to
  think it was.
- **Augustus's *Res Gestae* is lapidary in the literal sense**, composed to
  be cut into bronze and stone. Its Latin is terse, official, exact with
  numbers and offices, first person throughout: *"I built… I gave… I
  refused…"*. English that runs smooth softens the granite; the numerals and
  the refusals are the style.
- **Marcus's Greek is private.** The *Meditations* were notes to himself:
  compressed, repetitive, sometimes ungrammatical, thinking rather than
  prose (Hadot 1998; Brunt 1974). Casaubon unfolds these knots into rolling
  seventeenth-century periods. What is lost is not accuracy so much as
  texture: the reader of Casaubon cannot tell which sentences Marcus
  laboured and which he jotted.
- **Seneca's epigrams travel best.** His pointed, end-stopped sentence
  (built to be quoted) survives translation better than any other style in
  the Archive, which is partly why Seneca has always been the most quoted
  Roman in English.

## 5. The text beneath the translation

The Latin column is not bedrock either. Works that survived by copying carry
manuscripts that disagree; editors chose; a different editor would print a
different word in places. A parallel display can make the original look more
fixed than it is, so it is worth knowing how each text reached its editor:

- The **letters to Atticus** vanished for most of a millennium. Petrarch
  found a manuscript at Verona in 1345; that codex is lost, and the modern
  text descends from copies made after his find. We are reading a copy of a
  copy of a rediscovery, and only one side of it, since Atticus's replies do
  not survive.
- The ***Res Gestae*** survives as no manuscript at all: the original bronze
  at Rome is gone, and the text is assembled from stone copies in provincial
  cities, chiefly the bilingual inscription at Ankara (the *Monumentum
  Ancyranum*), with gaps restored by editorial conjecture from Mommsen
  (1883) to Cooley (2009). Some words a reader meets in the Archive's Latin
  column are a scholar's reconstruction of what the stone once said.
- The ***Meditations*** rests on one complete manuscript (Vaticanus graecus
  1950) and one lost one, from which Xylander printed the first edition in
  1559. Casaubon translated a text that was itself barely a century old in
  print.
- **Quintilian** circulated for centuries in a mutilated text until Poggio
  Bracciolini found a complete manuscript at St Gall in 1416, during a break
  in the Council of Constance. **Seneca's letters** travelled the Middle
  Ages split into two halves (1–88 and 89–124), reunited by print (Reynolds
  1965).

The texts themselves deserve confidence: their overall fidelity is one of
the triumphs of the manuscript tradition (Reynolds & Wilson 2013). What
needs precision is the phrase "parallel original". What stands in that
column is an editor's best reconstruction, with a history, beside a
translator's interpretation, with a date.

## 6. How the Archive should be read

Three practices follow:

1. **Treat the English as a guide to the original rather than a
   replacement.** When a passage matters, look across the columns; the
   friction between them is where the act of translation becomes visible,
   and the parallel corpus is most useful precisely there.
2. **Keep the translator's name attached.** The Archive shows it on every
   citation. "Casaubon's Marcus" and "Shuckburgh's Cicero" are accurate
   descriptions; "Marcus" and "Cicero" alone, for an English quotation, are
   not quite.
3. **Expect the seams, and value them.** A smooth surface would be a false
   one. The Archive does not try to hide the seams: it names every edition
   and translator, shows the original where it has one, and lets the reader
   see that reading Rome in English is reading Rome through other centuries'
   eyes. Read knowingly, that becomes a second subject of the encounter
   rather than a defect of it.
